Solar System

Mercury

Mercury, the smallest and innermost planet, orbits the Sun in just 88 Earth days. It has a diameter of about 4,880 kilometers (3,032 miles) and is roughly 38% the size of Earth. Due to its proximity to the Sun, temperatures on Mercury can reach a searing 430°C (800°F) during the day, but because it has almost no atmosphere to retain heat, nighttime temperatures drop dramatically to -180°C (-290°F). The planet’s surface is heavily cratered, resembling our Moon. Mercury has no moons and no rings. Its thin atmosphere, called an exosphere, is composed mainly of oxygen, sodium, and hydrogen.

Venus

Venus, often called Earth's "sister planet" due to its similar size and composition, is the second planet from the Sun. It has a thick, toxic atmosphere composed mainly of carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulfuric acid. This dense atmosphere traps heat, creating an intense greenhouse effect that makes Venus the hottest planet in the Solar System, with surface temperatures around 465°C (869°F), even hotter than Mercury. Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction to most planets, meaning it has a day (243 Earth days) that’s longer than its year (225 Earth days). Its surface is dotted with volcanoes and vast plains. Despite its harsh conditions, Venus has intrigued scientists for potential studies of atmospheric and surface processes.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to harbor life. It has a unique atmosphere, composed mostly of nitrogen and oxygen, which supports diverse ecosystems and protects the planet from harmful solar radiation. Earth has a moderate climate, liquid water on its surface, and a magnetic field that shields it from cosmic and solar radiation. The planet’s surface is 71% water, with the remaining 29% being continents and islands. Earth’s atmosphere and distance from the Sun make it perfect for sustaining life. It has one natural satellite, the Moon, which influences tides and helps stabilize the planet's axial tilt.

Mars

Mars, known as the "Red Planet," is the fourth planet from the Sun. Its reddish appearance is due to iron oxide (rust) on its surface. Mars has a thin atmosphere, primarily composed of carbon dioxide, with very low air pressure, making it impossible for liquid water to exist for long on the surface. However, evidence suggests that liquid water once flowed on Mars, and there may be water ice beneath its surface. The planet has seasons, polar ice caps, and the largest volcano in the Solar System, Olympus Mons, which stands nearly three times the height of Mount Everest. Mars has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos. Scientists are particularly interested in Mars because it shows potential signs of past life and could be a future destination for human exploration.

Jupiter

Jupiter, the fifth planet from the Sun, is the largest planet in the Solar System, more than twice as massive as all the other planets combined. It’s a gas giant, composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, and lacks a solid surface. Its atmosphere is famous for its colorful bands and the Great Red Spot, a giant storm that has raged for over 300 years. Jupiter has at least 79 moons, the largest of which—Ganymede, Callisto, Io, and Europa—are known as the Galilean moons. Ganymede is the largest moon in the Solar System. Jupiter's strong magnetic field and numerous moons make it an object of intense scientific study.

Saturn

Saturn, the sixth planet from the Sun, is renowned for its stunning ring system, which is made up of ice, dust, and rock particles. It’s a gas giant like Jupiter, composed mostly of hydrogen and helium. Saturn is the second-largest planet in the Solar System, with a diameter about nine times that of Earth. It has at least 83 moons, with Titan being the largest and even bigger than Mercury. Titan is particularly interesting because it has a thick atmosphere and lakes of liquid methane and ethane, making it a potential site for studying prebiotic chemistry. Saturn's rings and its complex moon system make it one of the most visually spectacular objects in the Solar System.

Uranus

Uranus, the seventh planet from the Sun, is an ice giant, composed mainly of water, ammonia, and methane ices, atop a small rocky core. Unlike other planets, Uranus rotates on its side, meaning it has extreme seasonal variations. Its pale blue color is due to the methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red light and reflects blue. Uranus has 27 known moons and a faint ring system. Its magnetic field is also unusual, as it is tilted at an angle different from its rotational axis. Uranus is one of the least explored planets, with only one spacecraft, Voyager 2, having flown by it in 1986.

Neptune

Neptune, the eighth and farthest known planet from the Sun, is also an ice giant. It is similar to Uranus in composition but has a deeper blue color due to its atmospheric composition. Neptune has the strongest winds in the Solar System, with speeds reaching up to 2,100 km/h (1,300 mph). It takes about 165 Earth years to complete one orbit around the Sun. Neptune has at least 14 moons, the largest being Triton, which is geologically active, with geysers that eject nitrogen gas into space. Like Uranus, Neptune also has a faint ring system. Despite its distance from the Sun, Neptune is still an active and dynamic planet.
